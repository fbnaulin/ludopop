I've seen two types of resolution in roleplaying games: as prompt, and as chance.

## As prompt
The game propose that players consult a randomizer based on character statistics. The result is usually modify game state, and the player is invited to incorporate or justify that change in the fiction. The result could help creativity.

Sometimes, player take narrative control elements outside her character. Gameplay here is more about looking for coherence, and cohesion, between a generative system and storytelling.

## As chance
A random result is compared with odds, after player has declarated a game change. Resolution only purpose is to verify if that declaration becomes true, or false.

Player is pushing the narrative, taking chances, betting. Usually, it's a game of all or nothing. Gameplay derivates in risk assessment.

## A brief comparison
In resolution as chance, player has less control, and the world is always an external and unpredictable force. Therefor, It's more vertiginous than the contemplative promt alternative. Both produce gameplay that demand effort, but in resolution as chance, the prompts are allocated as previous player proposition to resolution.

## Resolution in TLA
As observed in TLA combat rules, I have prefer chance, but instead true, or false outputs, it generates a dicotomy between quantitative (hit points reduction), and qualitative (wounds); intrinsic, and abstract versus situational, and concrete.

Now, I have to think about similar binary output for two new kind of interactions, core to the game: social-village, and travel-wilderness.
